Data, Information, and Information Systems

This portfolio examines the vitality of data and information systems. From transforming raw data into actionable insights to leveraging cutting-edge technology for organizational decision-making, these systems shape how businesses operate and grow. Dive into their components, applications, and the support systems that drive success across industries.

Data and Information

Data

Data refers to recorded descriptions of objects, events, activities, and transactions that have no specific meaning. It can be qualitative (involving categorical variables) or quantitative (involving numerical variables).

Information

Information refers to data that has been processed, transformed, and displayed to denote a specific meaning. All in all, information must be RELIABLE, TIMELY, ACCURATE, UNAMBIGUOUS, UNBIASED, and COMPLETE.

Differentiating data and information

Example of Data: numbers representing temperatures in a city taken at hourly intervals throughout the day

Example of Information: creating a weather report using the given temperatures in order to analyze today's weather patterns

Information Systems

Definition

An information system collects, processes, and analyzes data to support decision-making, coordination, and control within an organization. It integrates hardware, software, and people to turn data into valuable information and knowledge, driving organizational improvement.

Importance

Information systems are crucial for businesses to grow, offering better data storage, improved decision-making, and a competitive edge. Fueled by advancements in digitalization and intelligence, they help drive value in the digital economy.

Components of Information Systems

HARDWARE encompasses the tangible components of an information system, such as hard disks and keyboards.

SOFTWARE encompasses the set of instructions that tell the hardware what to execute.

DATA refers to the collection of facts that is gathered for decision-making purposes.

PEOPLE encompasses all humans involved in managing information systems, such as support staff, developers, and executive authorities.

TELECOMMUNICATIONS refers to the connection between computer networks that allow information to be shared and transmitted.

PROCESSES involve a series of steps that aim to achieve a desired goal.

Applications of Information Systems

Information Systems are present in all sorts of organizations and industries, from retail to education and politics. Information Systems are used to generate financial systems, improve Netflix recommendation systems, manage inventory and sales, organize patient information, record student attendance, store information about potential government candidates, and many more.

Pyramidal Model of Types of Support Systems in Information Systems

Here are the explanations of each type of support system, from the lowest hierarchy to the highest:

TPS | Transaction Processing System

Transaction Processing Systems (TPS) monitor, collect, store, and process data from basic business transactions, such as interactions between employees, customers, and suppliers. They continuously gather and organize data, often in real time, forming the foundation for other information systems like MIS. TPS is essential for core operations, with applications including payroll and reservation systems.

MIS | Management Information System

Management Information Systems (MIS) use data from TPS to help middle managers make informed decisions. They focus on providing reports on past and current performance, with limited analytical capabilities. MIS gives easy access to financial and marketing information. Applications include budgeting and sales management systems.

DSS | Decision Support System

Decision Support Systems (DSS) analyze existing information to help managers assess the potential effects of their decisions. Applications include logistics systems and financial planning systems.

EIS | Executive Information System

Executive Information Systems (EIS) help senior executives and management easily access information to make high-level decisions that align with the organization’s goals. They facilitate the flow of information from both internal and external sources and are tailored to analyze long-term trends relevant to the organization’s environment.
